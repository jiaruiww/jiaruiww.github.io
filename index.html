Job Market Paper

Welfare and the Act of Competing
Abstract | Paper | Protocol | oTree

Economists often view competition as a positive force that incentivizes effort and improves efficiency. Yet a full accounting of the welfare effects of competition requires consideration of the direct utility costs and benefits of competition itself. This paper investigates how competition affects utility derived purely from the act of competing, independent of realized outcomes. I conduct a series of experiments which show that competition shapes utility through two opposing channels: a belief channel, whereby competition lowers expectations of success, thus reducing belief utility, and an intrinsic utility channel, whereby indi- viduals derive enjoyment directly from competing. The overall effect depends on the relative strength of these channels. I also show that these utility effects can impact future choices: attribution bias in competitive settings, as it leads individuals to misattribute the enjoyment of competition to the underlying task.

Working Papers

Political Power and Collectivism: The Persistent Impact of Historical Political Regime
with Yuting Chen and Liqiang Liu
Abstract | Paper

Understanding the determinants of collectivism is essential, as it shapes economic behavior, institutional development, and social interactions. This paper identifies a novel determinant of collectivism: exposure to centralized political power. The identification compares individuals from the same birth cohort within a province, exploiting variation in county distances to historical Chinese national capitals, instrumented by proximity to the initial territorial centroids of ten centralized dynasties. Using data from the 2005 China census, we find that individuals residing in counties closer to historical capitals of centralized regimes exhibit stronger collectivist orientation than cohort peers in more distant counties within the same province. The finding is robust to alternative measures of collectivism and exposure to centralized regimes, more conservative inference approaches, and the inclusion of additional covariates. We also find that the effect varies across regions and between the periods before and after the PRC’s establishment in 1949, but not by gender.
