Job Market Paper

Welfare and the Act of Competing
Abstract | Paper | Protocol | oTree

This paper studies how the act of competing affects individual welfare beyond material payoffs. Using a novel experimental design, I examine whether individuals derive utility or disutility from the competitive process itself, independent of outcomes. Results suggest that competition has intrinsic value that varies significantly across individuals and contexts, with important implications for understanding labor market participation and tournament design.

Working Papers

Political Power and Collectivism: The Persistent Impact of Historical Political Regime
with Yuting Chen and Liqiang Liu
Abstract | Paper

This study investigates how historical political regimes shape contemporary collectivist values and behaviors. Using variation in historical governance structures across Chinese regions, we find that areas with stronger traditional collective institutions exhibit higher levels of cooperation and group-oriented decision making today, suggesting deep cultural persistence of institutional arrangements.

Risk Aversion and Costly Information Acquisition
with Jiaqi Yang
Abstract | Paper

We examine how risk preferences affect individuals' willingness to acquire costly information before making decisions under uncertainty. Through a series of laboratory experiments, we demonstrate that more risk-averse individuals are willing to pay higher costs for information, but this relationship is moderated by the precision and relevance of available information.

Pre-doc Research

Do Markets Erode Trust? An Experimental Study, with Yefeng Chen, Yuli Ding, Yiwen Pan, Xianghua Wu
Abstract | Paper

This experimental study investigates whether market interactions undermine interpersonal trust. Through a series of trust games embedded within market contexts, we find that while market participation can reduce trust in some settings, the effect depends critically on the transparency of market mechanisms and the presence of reputation systems.
